    # Skills For Defining A New Product Domain

This document defines the core skills needed to execute the workflow described in [README.md](./README.md) for creating a new product domain under `_config/product-domains/`.

The intent is to make the required capability set explicit before starting research, modeling, and JSON authoring.

## Core Skills

### Product Domain Framing
Ability to identify the most appropriate business domain to model, define clear boundaries for that domain, and distinguish core scope from adjacent businesses, supporting services, and enabling capabilities.

### Market Research
Ability to build an evidence-based view of the target company, category, competitors, substitutes, monetization model, customer behavior, and operating environment using public information and careful inference where facts are incomplete.

### Customer Segmentation
Ability to identify distinct customer groups, personas, users, buyers, operators, and beneficiaries, and separate them into meaningful segments with materially different goals, constraints, and buying logic.

### Jobs To Be Done Modeling
Ability to translate customer intent into structured jobs, desired outcomes, process steps, frictions, and success criteria so the model reflects customer progress rather than internal feature lists.

### Customer Journey Design
Ability to describe the end-to-end customer experience across trigger, discovery, evaluation, trial, engagement, and retention, including trust moments, drop-off risks, adoption barriers, and repeat-use drivers.

### Product Strategy
Ability to define product vision, strategic focus, differentiation, product themes, and 1-year, 3-year, and 5-year horizons grounded in customer value and business viability.

### Value Proposition Design
Ability to express why each segment would choose the product, which pains are removed, which gains are created, and which concrete outcomes make the offer compelling.

### Business Model Design
Ability to explain how the domain creates, delivers, and captures value, including revenue streams, pricing logic, marketplace economics where relevant, and the operational drivers behind business outcomes.

### Goal Setting
Ability to convert strategy into explicit objectives with measurable outcomes, balanced ambition, and clear success criteria for the domain and its major customer segments.

### KPI Architecture
Ability to design KPI pyramids from top-level north-star measures down to supporting and diagnostic metrics, linking customer outcomes to business outcomes with metrics that are specific, measurable, and useful for decisions.

### Strategic Planning
Ability to convert domain strategy into milestones, phases, planning assumptions, and horizon-based sequencing covering what should happen now, next, and later.

### Roadmap Design
Ability to shape initiatives and release sequencing into a coherent roadmap that reflects value delivery, dependencies, execution order, and risk reduction.

### Organizational Design
Ability to define the team and ownership model needed to execute the domain, including responsibilities, boundaries, coordination points, and alignment between product areas and accountable teams.

### Team Topology Design
Ability to structure teams around product areas, platforms, enabling functions, or streams of work in a way that reduces coordination overhead and keeps ownership understandable.

### Capability Mapping
Ability to define the product capabilities required to support customer jobs and business flows, and show how those capabilities contribute to customer outcomes and strategic priorities.

### Product Brick Architecture
Ability to decompose the domain into a durable three-level implementation-facing structure such as domain, group, and brick, with clean boundaries, realistic naming, and traceability to customer and business value.

### Solution and Systems Architecture
Ability to connect product capabilities to systems, APIs, events, integrations, and operational concerns at a practical level without drifting into speculative low-level implementation.

### Delivery Model Design
Ability to describe how the product is delivered through channels, interfaces, APIs, events, operational workflows, and release structures in a way that matches the repository's delivery modeling patterns.

### Evidence-Based Modeling
Ability to separate facts, assumptions, and inferred judgments, document uncertainty honestly, and still produce a coherent model when public information is incomplete.

### Schema And Repository Pattern Recognition
Ability to inspect existing domains and infer the current canonical structure, required files, JSON schemas, naming patterns, and modeling depth, especially where the repository includes both older and newer variants.

### Structured JSON Authoring
Ability to produce valid, internally consistent JSON aligned with repository conventions, including lowercase IDs, stable references, clean nesting, and consistent semantic depth.

### Static Documentation Modeling
Ability to create source content that remains clear and useful when rendered into the static documentation site, preserving consistency, navigability, and terminology.

### Architecture Skills

- Capability mapping
- Product brick architecture
- Solution and systems architecture
- Service, API, and event boundary reasoning

### Product Strategy Skills

- Category dynamics analysis
- Public-signal triangulation

- KPI architecture

- Release planning
- Dependency sequencing
- MVP and expansion path design

## Meta-Skills

### New Product Domain Definition
Combines market research, customer modeling, product strategy, KPI design, organization design, capability mapping, product brick architecture, and structured JSON authoring to create a complete new domain under `_config/product-domains/<domain>/`.

### Grounded Product Architecture Modeling
Combines customer needs, strategic goals, delivery structure, product capabilities, product bricks, teams, and planning artifacts into one coherent domain model that remains implementation-aware and repository-consistent.
